Freedom Fighter of Telangana

Komaram Bheem was a tribal revolutionary leader and folk hero who fought against the oppressive rule of the Nizam of Hyderabad in the early 20th century. He belonged to the Gond tribe in what is now Telangana.

Background of Komaram Bheem

< Komaram Bheem was born around 1901 in Sankepalli village (present-day Asifabad district, Telangana). He belonged to the Gond tribe, one of the largest Adivasi (tribal) communities in India. Bheem grew up witnessing the exploitation of tribal people by the Nizam’s landlords, forest officials, and police, who denied them basic rights over their own land and resources.

Struggle of Komaram Bheem

Komaram Bheem, a Gond leader, fought against the feudal system and British rule in Telangana during the 1930s. He led a rebellion against the Nizam of Hyderabad and the British Raj in the eastern part of the Princely State, contributing to the larger Telangana Rebellion. Bheem's movement was characterized by guerrilla warfare and his slogan "Jal, Jangal, Zameen" (Water, Forest, Land), symbolizing the fight against encroachment and exploitation.
Feudal Oppression: Bheem's movement was a response to the exploitative practices of the feudal landlords under the Nizam's rule. British Rule: He also opposed the British colonial administration's presence and control in the region. Guerrilla Warfare: Bheem and his followers engaged in a low-intensity guerrilla campaign, defying the Nizam's authority and living in the forests. "Jal, Jangal, Zameen": This slogan became a rallying cry for the rebellion, emphasizing the importance of water, forests, and land for the Adivasi (tribal) communities. Telangana Rebellion: Bheem's rebellion was a significant component of the larger Telangana Rebellion, which culminated in the Sayudha Porattam of 1946. Legacy: Bheem's death in 1940 was followed by his apotheosis as a symbol of rebellion and his continued influence in Gond culture and Adivasi movements. Further Context: Bheem is considered a prominent figure in the movement for Telangana statehood. His story is also featured in the movie RRR, which portrays him alongside Alluri Sitarama Raju, another freedom fighter. was again approached by forest officials who tried to force him to leave arguing that the land belonged to the state. Bheem then tried to lobby the Nizam directly and sought to present the grievances of the Adivasis before him but he received no response. Bheem then decided to engage in armed revolution. He formed clandestine associations with the banned Communist Party of India,and started mobilising the Adivasi population at Jodeghat, eventually calling a council of tribal leaders from the twelve traditional districts of Ankusapur, Bhabejhari, Bhimangundi, Chalbaridi, Jodeghat, Kallegaon, Koshaguda, Linepatter, Narsapur, Patnapur, Shivaguda and Tokennavada. The council decided to form a guerilla army to protect their lands. Bheem also proposed they declare themselves an independent Gond kingdom. Some see this as a predecessor to more recent attempts to form an autonomous Gondwana.
The council was followed by an uprising in the Gondi region which began in 1928. The forces mobilised to attack the zamindars in Babejhari and Jodeghat. In response, the Nizam recognised Bheem as leader of the Gond rebels and sent the collector at Asifabad to negotiate with him, offering assurances of land grants to the Gonds. Bheem rejected the initial offer stating that they sought justice and instead demanded regional autonomy for the Gonds, eviction of the forest officials and zamindars, and the release of all Gond prisoners in the penal system of Hyderabad state. The demands were rejected and the conflict continued as a low intensity guerilla campaign over the following decade. Bheem directly commanded 300 men under him and operated out of Jodeghat. He is said to have coined the slogan Jal, Jangal, Zameen (transl. Water, Forest, Land) during this period. Bheem's whereabouts were eventually discovered by Kurdu Patel and he was killed in an encounter with armed policemen led by the talukdar of Asifabad, Abdul Sattar. Fifteen others were killed in the encounter.[5] The date of his death is disputed, it's officially recognised to have occurred in October 1940 but Gondi people commemorate it on 8 April 1940.
