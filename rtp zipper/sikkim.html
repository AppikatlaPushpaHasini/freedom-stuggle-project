Freedom Fighter of Sikkim

Tashi Namgyal was a prominent figure in the history of Sikkim, particularly known for his role as the Chogyal (King) of Sikkim. He ruled the small Himalayan kingdom from 1914 to 1963.

Background of Tashi Namgyal

Tashi Namgyal was born in 1893 and was the eldest son of Chogyal Thutob Namgyal. He became the ruler of Sikkim in 1914 after the passing of his father, King Thutob Namgyal. As a ruler, Tashi Namgyal continued the work of his father in maintaining the sovereignty of Sikkim, which at the time had close ties to both British India and Tibet.

Struggle of Tashi Namgyal

While Sikkim Tashi Namgyal's followers initially supported closer ties with India and eventually favored integration with the Indian Union, their opposition to British influence was less direct. They primarily focused on gaining autonomy and a more democratic system within Sikkim, which ultimately led to their support for India's influence as a way to achieve those goals.
Support for Indian Influence and Integration: Discontent with Monarchy: Growing resentment against the monarchy, particularly its feudal control and perceived inequality, fueled the desire for a more democratic and responsible government. Sikkim State Congress: In 1947, political groups formed the Sikkim State Congress, advocating for the merging of Sikkim with the Union of India. Indo-Sikkim Pact: In 1950, a treaty was signed, granting Sikkim protectorate status under India, with India controlling defense, external affairs, and communications. Demand for Responsible Government: The Sikkim State Congress, along with other groups, continued to demand a responsible government and greater autonomy, even after the protectorate status. Referendum and Integration: In 1975, a referendum was held, and a large majority of voters supported abolishing the monarchy and Sikkim becoming part of the Indian Union. Opposition to British Influence (Indirectly): Seeking Indian Assistance: The Sikkim monarchy, including Palden Thondup Namgyal, sought assistance from India to quell internal dissent and maintain control, effectively relying on Indian influence to suppress opposition, according to Wikipedia. Internal Political Tensions: The desire for greater autonomy and the monarchy's attempts to maintain control led to internal political tensions and anti-monarchist movements, further pushing Sikkim towards Indian influence. Autonomy and Trade Interests: The demand for autonomy, initially focused on security, later shifted to economic and trade interests, further demonstrating the desire for greater control over Sikkim's affairs, according to The Indian Express. British Influence in the Past: Before the Indo-Sikkim Pact, British influence had been established through treaties and agreements, making Sikkim a protectorate of the British Raj. Tashi Namgyal's followers, by advocating for closer ties with India, effectively sought to replace British influence with Indian influence in Sikkim. While some within Sikkim supported the Indian independence movement and opposed British influence, particularly after 1947, the situation was complex and not universally shared. Tashi Namgyal, the Chogyal (ruler) of Sikkim, signed a treaty with India in 1950, establishing Sikkim as a protectorate of India. This decision, while aiming to ensure Sikkim's security and autonomy, also involved a degree of reliance on India, which some may have seen as a continuation of British influence, albeit under a different banner. Here's a more detailed look: Sikkim as a Protectorate: After India's independence, Sikkim became a protectorate of India, with a treaty signed in 1950 between Tashi Namgyal and the Indian Political Officer. This meant that Sikkim, while retaining a degree of autonomy in internal affairs, was still under Indian protection and influence. Internal Dynamics: Within Sikkim, there were different perspectives on its relationship with India and the legacy of British influence. Some, particularly among the Bhutia and Lepcha communities, may have felt threatened by the situation in Tibet and sought protection and support from India. Emergence of Nationalism: A growing desire for autonomy and greater control over its affairs emerged in Sikkim, fueled by issues like border disputes with China and India's military presence in the region. This led to demands for a larger voice in dealing with the outside world, particularly in areas like tourism and trade. Referendum and Integration: In 1975, after internal political tensions and demonstrations, the Indian army took control of Gangtok, and a referendum was held. The overwhelming majority voted in favor of abolishing the monarchy and joining India, effectively ending Sikkim's status as a protectorate and making it a constituent state of India. In essence, while Tashi Namgyal's followers may have had varying views on the relationship with India and the legacy of British influence, the decision to make Sikkim a protectorate, followed by its eventual integration into India, reflected a complex interplay of political, strategic, and internal factors. The history of Sikkim begins with the indigenous Lepcha's contact with early Tibetan settlers. Historically, Sikkim was a sovereign monarchical State in the eastern Himalayas. Later a protectorate of India followed by a merger with India and official recognition as a state of India. Lepchas were the main inhabitants as well as the Rulers of the land up to 1641. Lepchas are generally considered to be the first people, Indigenous to Sikkim also includes Darjeeling.The establishment of the Buddhist kingdom under the Chogyal in the 17th century was followed by British rule in Sikkim and thereafter inclusion in India as an official state of the nation post- independence. Sikkim emerged as a polity in its own right against a backdrop of incursions from Tibet and Bhutan, during which the kingdom enjoyed varying degrees of independence. In the early 18th century, the British Empire sought to establish trade routes with Tibet, leading Sikkim to fall under British suzerainty until independence in 1947. Initially, Sikkim remained an independent country, until it merged with India in 1975. Many provisions of the Indian constitution had to be altered to accommodate the international treaties and between Sikkim and India.The 1979 assembly election saw Nar Bahadur Bhandari elected Chief Minister of Sikkim. Bhandari held on to win again in 1984 and 1989. In 1994, Assembly politician Pawan Kumar Chamling became the Chief Minister of Sikkim. In 1999, 2004, 2009 and 2014 Chamling consolidated his position to sweep the polls. Sikkim voted for its one delegate to the All India Lok Sabha on 3 January 1980. The voters strongly favoured a local listing party, rejecting thecandidates from the Janata and Congress. Sino-Indian relations were somewhat bruised in 2000 by an event in Sikkim that challenged China's longstanding claim of Sikkim as an independent country. The unusual event was the escape of Ogyen Trinley Dorje from Tibet to Dharamsala, India. Ogyen Trinley Dorje is one of the two rival claimants who seek recognition as the 17th Karmapa, the head of the Black Hat branch of Tibetan Buddhism (see Karmapa controversy). The two claimants did battle in the Indian court system for control of the considerable funds collected by the 16th Karmapa for the restoration and maintenance of Rumtek Monastery, located in Gangtok, Sikkim. The Chinese, who recognise Ogyen Trinley Dorje as the true Karmapa, were unhappy about the court outcome which awarded the monastery funds to the other rival. However, the Chinese government was in a quandary as to what to do about it, as any protest to India would mean an explicit endorsement that the high court of India holds jurisdiction over Sikkim. In 2003, with the thawing of relations between the two nations, Indian sovereignty over Sikkim was finally recognised by China. The two governments also proposed to open the Nathula and Jelepla Passes in 2005. On 18 September 2011, a magnitude 6.9Mw earthquake struck Sikkim, killing at least 116 people in the state and Nepal, Bhutan, Bangladesh and Tibet, China. More than 60 people died in Sikkim alone, and the city of Gangtok suffered significant damage.
