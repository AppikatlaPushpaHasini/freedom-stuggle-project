Freedom Fighter of chattigarh

Veer Narayan Singh born in 1795 in Sonakhan, located in the present-day Chhattisgarh region. Belonged to a Zamindar family of the Binjhwar tribal community, known for their warrior spirit and loyalty to their people. Became the Zamindar of Sonakhan and was deeply respected by locals for his fairness and leadership.

Background of Veer Narayan Singh

Veer Narayan Singh is considered the first freedom fighter from Chhattisgarh. In 1856, during a severe famine, he raided British grain stores to distribute food to starving villagers. This act of defiance made him a hero among the poor and a target of the British. He was arrested by the British for this act of rebellion.

Struggle of Veer Narayan Singh

Veer Narayan Singh, a landlord from Chhattisgarh, led a tribal revolt against British rule in 1857, drawing inspiration from the broader Indian revolt of 1857. He formed an army of 500 men and fought against the British, but was eventually arrested and executed for sedition.
Revolt: Veer Narayan Singh was part of the larger Indian rebellion against British rule, which was sparked by various factors, including military issues (like the use of greased cartridges) and a growing sense of discontent with British policies. Forming a Tribal Army: He rallied local tribal communities and formed an army of 500 men, likely drawn from the peasant population and other disgruntled individuals. Escaping Prison: After being arrested in 1856 for distributing food during a famine, he escaped jail with the help of British soldiers at Raipur and returned to Sonakhan, where he organized the revolt. Fighting the British: He led his forces against the British army, which was commanded by Deputy Commissioner Smith. Capture and Execution: Despite fierce fighting, Veer Narayan Singh's forces were eventually defeated, and he was recaptured and executed on charges of sedition. Why he fought: British Exploitation: The revolt was a direct response to British exploitation of the region and its people, including economic policies that led to famine and poverty. Patriotism and Resistance: The revolt also reflected a growing sense of Indian nationalism and a desire to resist foreign rule.
Social Justice: Veer Narayan Singh's actions, such as distributing food during the famine, also reflected a desire for social justice and an attempt to alleviate the suffering of the poor.
