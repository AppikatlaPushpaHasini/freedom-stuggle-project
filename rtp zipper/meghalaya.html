Freedom Fighter of Meghalaya

U Tirot Sing was a revered and brave leader of the Khasi tribe, an indigenous community in the northeastern region of India, specifically in Meghalaya. He is best known for his role in resisting British colonial expansion in the early 19th century, especially for leading his people in the Anglo-Khasi War (also known as the Khasi Uprising) in the years 1829–1833. U Tirot Sing's legacy is one of valor, sacrifice, and resistance to foreign domination, and he is remembered as a national hero in the region.

Background of U Tirot Sing

Birth: U Tirot Sing was born in the late 18th century, in the Khasi Hills region, which is now part of Meghalaya, India. The Khasi people were known for their unique matrilineal society, where lineage and inheritance passed through the mother’s side. He was born into a royal family and became the Syiem (chief) of the Khasi tribe of Nongkhlaw, a region located within the Khasi Hills. He was well-educated and knowledgeable about his tribe's customs, traditions, and history, which helped him become a respected and influential leader.

Struggle of U Tirot Sing

U Tirot Sing, a Khasi ruler, led a revolt against British rule in the Khasi Hills by attacking the British garrison in Nongkhlaw, resulting in the deaths of two British officers. This action sparked the Anglo-Khasi War (1829-1833), in which he and his followers fought using guerrilla tactics against the British forces. Despite their bravery, the Khasis were unable to match the British firepower, and Tirot Sing was eventually captured, deported, and died in captivity.
Initial Attack: On April 4, 1829, Tirot Sing's forces attacked the British garrison at Nongkhlaw, killing two officers and triggering the Anglo-Khasi War. Guerilla Warfare: Unable to match the British in open battle, Tirot Sing and his people resorted to guerrilla tactics, using their knowledge of the terrain to their advantage and launching surprise attacks. British Counteroffensive: The British retaliated with military operations, eventually forcing Tirot Sing to hide in caves. Capture and Deportation: In January 1833, Tirot Sing was betrayed and captured by the British. He was then deported to Dhaka, Bangladesh, and died in captivity on July 17, 1835. Legacy: Tirot Sing's bravery and resistance against the British are still remembered in Meghalaya, where his death anniversary is commemorated as a state holiday. The British had gained control over the Brahmaputra valley after concluding the Treaty of Yandabo in 1826.[2] Between their possessions in Sylhet and the newly acquired possessions in Lower Assam intervened the Khasi Hills. They wanted to construct a road through this area to connect Guwahati with Sylhet to save weeks of travel and malarious country. David Scott, the agent to the British Governor-General for the Northern Territory, found out that U Tirot Sing was interested in regaining possessions in the duars (passes into Assam) in return for the permission for the road project. After a two-day session of the durbar (court), the assembly agreed to the proposal of the British. Work on the road started. When Balaram Singh, Raja of Ranee, disputed U Tirot Sing's claims to the duars, he went with a party of armed men in December 1828 to establish his claim. He was confident that the British would support him; instead, he was confronted by a party of sepoys who blocked his passage.[1] When news came that the British were reinforcing forces in Assam, U Tirot Sing convened a Durbar again and passed orders for the British to evacuate Nongkhlaw. The British did not pay any heed,[4] and the Khasis attacked the British garrison in Nongkhlaw on 4 April 1829.[2] His men killed at his orders two British officers, Richard Gurdon Bedingfield and Philip Bowles Burlton.[5][6] In retaliation, British military operations began against U Tirot Sing and other Khasi chiefs. In the Anglo-Khasi War, the Khasis lacked firearms and had only swords, shields, bows and arrows. They were untrained in the British type of warfare and soon found that it was impossible to engage in open battle against an enemy who could kill from a distance. Therefore, they resorted to guerrilla activity, which dragged on for about four years.[1] Tirot Sing fought with native weapons such as a sword and shield. He was shot at by the British and had to hide in a cave and tend for his wound. He was eventually captured by the British in January 1833 and deported to Dhaka. The location of his hiding place was given by a chief of his who was bribed with gold coins by the British. He died on 17 July 1835. His death anniversary is commemorated every year as a state holiday in Meghalaya.
