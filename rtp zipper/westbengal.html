Freedom Fighter of Westbengal

Subhas Chandra Bose was one of the most influential and charismatic leaders in the Indian independence movement. He is best known for his radical approach to achieving India's freedom from British colonial rule. His legacy as a freedom fighter, nationalist, and visionary continues to inspire many people around the world. Here's an overview of his life and contributions:

Background of Subhas Chandra Bose

Born: January 23, 1897, in Cuttack, Odisha (then part of Bengal). Family: Bose came from a prominent Bengali family. His father, Janakinath Bose, was a lawyer, and his mother, Prabhavati Devi, was a devout woman who had a significant influence on him. Education: Bose was an intelligent and academically gifted student. He studied at Presidency College in Kolkata and later went to Cambridge University in England. He passed the Indian Civil Services (ICS) exam in 1920, a prestigious examination conducted by the British. However, his passion for India's independence led him to resign from this promising career and return to India.

Struggle of Subhas Chandra Bose

Subhas Chandra Bose - Led the Indian National Army (INA) against British rule.Subhas Chandra Bose, a prominent figure in the Indian independence movement, played a key role in forming and leading the Indian National Army (INA), also known as the Azad Hind Fauj, against British rule. He aimed to liberate India from British colonial rule through military means, and the INA was a significant part of his strategy.
Key aspects of Bose's role: Leadership: Bose, a leader of the Indian National Congress, advocated for a more militant approach to fight against the British, rather than negotiation or compromise. INA Formation: After escaping British house arrest, he sought support from the Axis powers, particularly Japan, to form the INA. Nationalist Vision: Bose envisioned the INA as a crucial tool to trigger a nationalist revolt against British rule in India. Radio Broadcasts: He used radio broadcasts to urge Indian communities in Southeast Asia to participate in the freedom struggle. Legacy: The INA, under Bose's leadership, became a symbol of Indian resistance against British rule and contributed to the broader movement for independence. Indian National Army (INA)’s March towards Delhi Subhas Chandra Bose believed the INA was ready to liberate India after several months of intense military training. His aim was to travel via Burma to reach north-east India. The Provisional Government of Azad Hind and the INA began deploying to Burma in January 1944. In March, the INA and the Japanese successfully conducted two military campaigns, capturing Imphal and Arakan. Following that, the Japanese decided to take Kohima, a key location along India’s hilly border with Burma. The INA and Japanese, on the other hand, took longer than planned to seize it. The INA and Japanese, who had run out of supplies by May, were forced to retire as the monsoons came. The war’s tide had shifted. Japan surrendered on August 15, 1945. Bose remained unfazed despite the loss of an ally. He went straight to the Soviet Union for help. Bose quickly appealed for help from the Soviet Union with the loss of the Japanese alliance. On 18 August 1945, however, his plane collapsed while on his way to the Soviet Union through Taiwan. Various sources afterward argued that the aircraft did not crash and that Bose reached the Soviet Union safely. The Trials of the Red Fort Following WWII, the British apprehended around 23,000 INA soldiers and charged them with treason. In November 1945, the INA trials began in the Red Fort. The first three senior INA officers, SN Khan, PK Sahgal, and GS Dhillon, became symbols of India’s fight for independence. Protests over the trials erupted, and the Royal Indian Navy and Royal Indian Air Force mutinied in February and March 1946. By that time, the end of British authority in India had become evident, and on August 15, 1947, India was declared free of colonial control.Indian National Army was a group with a variety of objectives. Its purpose was to bolster and fill out Japanese claims of establishing a Greater Asia co-prosperity zone. The INA was also formed to promote the development of armed Indian nationalism. The INA was created to weaken the British Indian Army, which was the backbone of British imperial power in the Far East.
The military participation of the INA was insignificant. On the Burma-India border, the army fought one significant battle, the Battle of Imphal-Kohima, but was beaten piecemeal. Its contribution to the cause of Indian nationalism, on the other hand, was far from insignificant. The British colonial administration planned to try the remaining members of the INA for treason. The fact that the trials would be held at the Red Fort sparked a fresh wave of nationalism in the country, as the Indian populace regarded the members as patriots fighting for freedom. The trials brought the British into nothing short of an imperial dilemma. The trial also resulted in a rebellion inside the UK Indian Army, with the Royal Indian Navy being particularly prominent. This led the British to understand that they had lost the military’s backing, and so their departure from India was inevitable.
