Freedom Fighter of Punjab

Bhagat Singh was one of the most iconic and influential revolutionaries in the Indian independence movement. He became a symbol of fearless resistance against British colonial rule and remains a youth icon in India to this day.

Background of Bhagat Singh

Born: September 28, 1907 Place: Banga, Punjab Province, British India (now in Pakistan) Family: Came from a patriotic Sikh family; many of his relatives were freedom fighters.

Struggle of Bhagat Singh

Bhagat Singh, a prominent figure in India's freedom struggle, fought against British rule through revolutionary activities and was executed in 1931. He was a key member of the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) and advocated for armed resistance. His notable acts included the bombing of the Central Legislative Assembly in 1929, a symbolic act to protest oppressive laws, and the assassination of John Saunders in 1928.
Key aspects of Bhagat Singh's revolutionary activities: Assassination of J.P. Saunders: Bhagat Singh and his associates, including Rajguru and Chandra Shekhar Azad, assassinated British police officer J.P. Saunders in 1928 to avenge the death of Lala Lajpat Rai. Central Assembly Bombing: In 1929, Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt threw bombs in the Central Assembly to protest repressive laws, without causing any casualties. They raised pro-revolutionary slogans and leaflets. Hunger Strike: In prison, Bhagat Singh and his associates, including Sukhdev and Rajguru, went on a hunger strike to protest the treatment of political prisoners. Bhagat Singh's legacy: His execution at the young age of 23, on March 23, 1931, made him a martyr, inspiring generations to fight against oppression. He is considered a symbol of courage and sacrifice in the Indian freedom struggle. Delhi Assembly bombing and arrest For some time, Bhagat Singh had been exploiting the power of drama as a means to inspire the revolt against the British, purchasing a magic lantern to show slides that enlivened his talks about revolutionaries such as Ram Prasad Bismil who had died as a result of the Kakori conspiracy. In 1929, he proposed a dramatic act to the HSRA intended to gain massive publicity for their aims. Influenced by Auguste Vaillant, a French anarchist who had bombed the Chamber of Deputies in Paris, Singh's plan was to explode a bomb inside the Central Legislative Assembly. The nominal intention was to protest against the Public Safety Bill, and the Trade Dispute Act, which had been rejected by the Assembly but were being enacted by the Viceroy using his special powers; the actual intention was for the perpetrators to allow themselves to be arrested so that they could use court appearances as a stage to publicise their cause.
