Freedom Fighter of Manipur

Rani Gaidinliu was an iconic Naga freedom fighter and spiritual leader who is remembered for her brave and tireless efforts in leading the Naga people’s resistance against British colonial rule. Known as the “Queen of the Nagas”, she became a symbol of strength and courage in the Indian independence struggle, particularly for the indigenous communities of Nagaland. Below is an overview of her life and legacy:

Background of Rani Gaidinliu

Born: January 26, 1915, in Nungshang Village, near Tengnoupal, in present-day Manipur, India. Gaidinliu was born into a family of the Zeliangrong Naga tribe. Her early life was steeped in the traditions and beliefs of her community. She was influenced by her surroundings and grew up with a strong sense of cultural identity and pride.

Struggle of Rani Gaidinliu

Rani Gaidinliu, a prominent figure in India's freedom struggle, led a tribal uprising against the British in Manipur and Nagaland, specifically during the Heraka movement. Following her cousin Haipou Jadonang's execution, she assumed leadership and transformed the movement from a religious revival into a political and military resistance against British rule.
Early Involvement: At the age of 13, Rani Gaidinliu joined the Heraka movement led by her cousin Haipou Jadonang, which aimed to revive traditional Naga customs and resist British influence. Assuming Leadership: After Jadonang's execution in 1931, Gaidinliu took over the leadership of the movement and transformed it into a full-blown political and military resistance. Mobilizing Resistance: She mobilized her people, encouraging them to resist British rule, reject colonial laws, and refuse to pay taxes. British Response: The British government sought to capture Gaidinliu and announced rewards for information about her, but she evaded capture and continued her resistance. Arrest and Imprisonment: In 1932, Gaidinliu was arrested by the British and sentenced to life imprisonment. Inspiration and Legacy: Despite her imprisonment, Gaidinliu's bravery inspired many in the freedom struggle, and she remained a steadfast symbol of resistance. She was eventually released in 1947 after India gained independence. Continued Activism: After her release, she continued to advocate for Naga culture and the rights of her people. She was born on January 26, 1915, in the present-day Tamenglong district of Manipur and died in 1993. She was the Naga spiritual and political leader who belonged to the Rongmei tribe (also known as Kabui). She led an armed uprising against the British in Manipur, Nagaland and Assam In 1927, at the age of 13, Gaidinliu, along with her cousin Haipou Jadonang, joined the Heraka movement, which aimed at the revival of the Naga tribal religion and establishing self-rule of the Nagas (Naga Raj) ending the British rule. She was arrested in 1932 when she was just 16 and was sentenced to life imprisonment by the British rulers. Between 1932 and 1947, the Naga freedom fighter was kept at several prisons across the Northeast. After being released in 1947 she continued to work for the betterment of the community. She passed away on February 17, 1993, at her native village Luangkao.Jawaharlal Nehru called her the “Daughter of the Hills” and gave her the title “Rani” or queen acknowledging her role in the struggle against the British.
In 1972, she was awarded Tamara Patra by the Government of India for her contribution to the freedom movement. She also received the Padma Bhushan in 1982. In 1996, the Union government issued a commemorative postage stamp in her honour. Vivekananda Sewa Summan in 1983, and Stree Shakti Puraskar in 1991. She posthumously was awarded the Bhagwan Birsa Munda Puraskar in 1996. The Government of India also issued a commemorative stamp in her honour in the same year. The Centre also released a Rs 100 commemorative coin in 2015 on the occasion of her 100th birth anniversary. The Indian Coast Guard commissioned a Fast Patrol Vessel “ICGS Rani Gaidinliu” in 2016. Recently, the Union Home Minister laid the foundation stone for the ‘Rani Gaidinliu Tribal Freedom Fighters Museum’ in Manipur’s Tamenglong district.
